Exquisite

Wield the power of cognitive science for seamless vocabulary learning.

Case Study: Human-Centered Design, iOS Development, Sketch

Originally created in a 5-week sprint with a multidisciplinary team, this iOS app has since been redesigned. In between work and classes, I taught myself iOS development to build this app and better understand mobile development. It's scheduled to release in the fall! Check out the latest prototype below!



Original Vision


Problem:
Users define unknown words only to forget them afterwards.

Angle:
What if when you defined a word, it was automatically added to a study list and it was intermittently quizzed until you learned it?

Implementation:
A mobile vocabulary app that uses contextual action to define and save words. Select an unknown word and 'share' it to Exquisite share/action extension wherein it is defined and added to a studylist. From there, the app sends notifications to remind the user to study or recall the word definition.

Muddled Vision

Our original vision solved a simple problem that users validated. We considered adding flashcard functionality, but it was cursory to the core of the app. Our instructor, however, was enamored by the prospect of a system of flexible, shareable, annotated flashcards. The instructor was excited by our project, so we didn't second guess it. To users, Exquisite became a flashcard app with the ability to contextually save words.

The instructor loved our final project -- so we did too. A year later, I decided to pursue development for learning purposes. I explained what the project was to friends and family, but I could never quite convey exactly what Exquisite was or why it should exist. But it hit me: Exquisite was basically Quizlet with one new feature. Yikes.

I made a couple of mistakes. One, I put too much stock in one piece of feedback. But worse -- I built most of it before I noticed. It was an app for casual vocabulary learners, yet the most considered feedback was from my professor who wanted a new teaching tool. The professor envisioned Quizlet, but it already existed. And Exquisite didn't match up favorably. It became more obvious as I looked at the bland final class prototype. Exquisite needed to be redesigned.

The Redesign

First, I spoke to users. Then, I sat down to clarify the problem, the user, and the product.

Old Problem:
Users define unknown words only to forget them afterwards.

New Problem:
Users don't want to study to expand their vocabulary.

Who are the users?

  • People who must communicate effectively.

  • People who prioritize self-improvement.

  • People who don't have time to dedicate to vocabulary.

  • Students, Writers, Speakers, Designers, Businesspeople.

  • Think: they would like to expand vocabulary

  • Feel: like studying vocabulary is a low leverage activity. High effort for low reward.

  • Do: not study vocabulary, but learn new words in daily life.

Product Value Proposition: With Exquisite you can seamlessly curate and effortlessly learn vocabulary.



Translating Value Proposition into Design Goals

Achieve these goals and the value proposition is fulfilled. Minimal Viable Product achieves each.

  1. Seamlessly Curate Words

    • Defining and saving words in (and outside of) the app is quick, simple, and easy to discover.

  2. Effortlessly Learn Words

    • Utilize cognitive science of learning and memory principles to maximize learning.

    • Interface requires minimal cognitive overhead to process information and navigate. Cognitive Effort is focused on the words.

    • Users don’t have to remember to study these words. They don’t need formal study sessions. Learning occurs in seamless and emergent intervals.

Implementation


Goal 1: Effortlessly Learn Words

First Implementation: Mastery System

The mastery system is a reflection of how well the user knows their words. Each word has three ratings: don't know 👎 , somewhat know👌, and mastered 👍. Each list has a mastery % which is calculated by how well the user has mastered each word in the list. The Mastery system not only informs the user what they need to learn, but also informs the Exquisite app as to what it needs to teach the user.


Second Implementation: Context Keyboard

We already learn vocabuary words without studying. How? Through context! The Context Keyboard uses the context of sentences that you type to teach your vocabulary words. It analyzes the context of what you type and suggests a word that makes sense from your Exquisite list in the QuickType bar. Using a word in context is an effective way to learn it!



Previous Next


  1. Type as normal, when the keyboard recognizes a context in which one of your vocabulary words fits, it will suggest it to you in green with a subtle audible “pop”.
  2. Tap the word to see the definition (it scrolls) and ensure the word makes sense in the context and for a brief ‘study moment’.
  3. If the word makes sense, tap it again to insert the word. Else, tap above the keyboard to dismiss.

Most users use the standard iOS keyboard. Hence, the Context Keyboard is designed to look and feel like the standard Apple iOS keyboard, but with the functionality of contextual vocabulary learning augmenting the standard QuickType bar. User interviews indicated that users lack patience with different keyboards. Therefore, the keyboard must work flawlessly. No slowdowns nor crashes, else the user will abandon it.


But It’s unlikely that a user will learn all of their vocabulary words with the Context Keyboard. Exquisite tracks usage metrics for each word and feeds them to an algorithm that prioritizes unused and unknown words for Microquiz Notifications.


Third Implementation: Microquiz Notifications

Exquisite's notifications are microquizzes that you do on your time. No need to open the app. Microquizzes are completed in the notification center. But they don't interrupt you. No sound, no vibration. They are designed to not be annoying. Microquiz notifications are integral to the app, and are thoughtfully designed as such.


In the span of a few seconds...
  1. User taps and holds notification banner.
  2. User answers multiple choice question.
  3. User taps and holds feedback notification banner.
  4. User responds with mastery rating of the word.

Lists Reworked

Every list is different in terms of content and user goals. That’s why exquisite lists have settings! They're located in the new list dashboard.

Proper list settings are critical to Exquisite's effectiveness. But I found that users didn't grasp which settings would yield desired results. As a result, you'll notice the "Learn List By:" setting. If the user is taking the GRE on June 1st, simply enter that date and exquisite will tailor a study plan of notifications towards meeting that goal! If no specific date is given, list settings can be dissociated. List settings control Notifications and basic study reminders. Every word shows up in the context keyboard. Navigating lists has changed too. Before, lists were toggled through search. That was clumsy and confusing. Now, tap the blue list title at the top. Select to view the list or study its flashcards. The list has a triangle dropdown indicator to signify the title's affordance of list changing.

Visual Design


The goal for the visual design is to focus on the words. The user's cognitive effort is to be focused on the words, not the interace. That being said, the interface isn't as bland as a dictionary page. Each word is on a virtual flashcard for visual relief. You'll see bits of color to highlight some relevant items and interactions. AlsNo function is hidden behind a swipe or gesture without a visual button or hint to accompany it because usability is prioritized. Swipes and gestures are used as accelerators.



Flashcards

The more words, the more difficult it is to learn them without studying. And for students who need to learn a large volume quickly, there are flashcards. They aren't annotations, but the mastery system is integrated during studying. The 'eye' icon toggles the viewing mode. Tap the 'eye' and the word is by itself. Tap the card and the definition is on the back. The 'shuffle' icon randomizes the order of the words.

Monetization

The app will be monetized through in-app purchases of curated word lists. For example, the GRE study list will include about 1000 curated and defined vocabulary words. Free curated lists will also be available.



Some Learnings

  • Engineering capabilities can constrain design direction. It's important to know the capabilities of the technologies utilized. It can dictate anything from major functions to minor styling.

  • Intentionally lofty goals can lead to novel solutions. These are useful even if not entirely achieved. I accomplished more than I otherwise would have.

  • Constantly question my assumptions and reasoning behind design decisions.
